# Taiwan Long-term Care Development

> **30-Second Overview:** Taiwan operates Asia's most comprehensive long-term care system, serving 360,000 people with 85% satisfaction rate. Yet simultaneously, 214,000 foreign caregivers support another, larger care ecosystem in private homes. This is a story about institutional design, social values, and the compromises of reality.

In 2017, the Tsai Ing-wen government launched Long-Term Care 2.0, promising to build a "continuous, universal long-term care service system." That same year, Taiwan employed over 200,000 foreign domestic caregivers for the first time.

This wasn't coincidence—it's Taiwan's greatest care paradox: one system funded with NT$65 billion annually serves 360,000 people through government programs, while another invisible system of 214,000 foreign caregivers serves approximately 210,000 people in private homes. Two parallel care worlds that barely interact.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's most counterintuitive policy achievement: building a world-class long-term care system while creating the world's most foreign caregiver-dependent society.

## The Birth of a System: Racing Against Super-Aging

In 1993, Taiwan's population aged 65 and above reached 7%, officially entering an aging society. Yet politicians reacted surprisingly slowly—it took 14 years until 2007 for the "Ten-Year Long-Term Care Plan" to launch.

This belated program was doomed from the start. A budget of NT$81.7 billion over 10 years looked substantial but averaged only NT$8 billion annually—less than 2% of the National Health Insurance budget. Worse, it served merely 20,000 people, covering just 1.5% of those in need.

By the 2016 presidential election, long-term care had become unavoidable for all three candidates. Tsai Ing-wen promised an upgrade to "Long-Term Care 2.0," switching from annual budgets to tobacco tax funding, boosting resources to over NT$30 billion annually.

> **⚠️ Controversial View**
> Medical associations criticized the plan: Japan's care insurance, funded half by taxes and half by insurance premiums, still faces severe shortfalls. Taiwan's tax-only approach raises sustainability concerns.

| 29,000 in 2017 | 360,000 in 2024 |
|---|---|
| First-year users of LTC 2.0 | Current users (12x growth) |

But impressive statistics masked harsh realities.

## The Great Labor Shortage: 7 Caregivers Rotating for 1 Client

"Can't find people" is the care system's biggest pain point. Tu Hsin-ning from the Taiwan Home Care Strategic Alliance notes structural collapse in human resources: in 2016, Taiwan had 186 home care units averaging 47.9 caregivers each; by 2024, there were 2,235 units averaging just 24.3 caregivers each.

More units, dispersed staff, inability to schedule flexibly. Chen Wan-yu, a case manager at Taipei's Junwei Home Care Institution, encountered an extreme case: one client needed seven different caregivers—sometimes different people for different time slots in a single day. One for bathing, another for walks, yet another for positioning.

> **💡 Did You Know?**
> Night shift home care is almost impossible to find. Despite government offering NT$385 night differential pay, few caregivers accept evening work. Working families get little respite after returning home.

More critically, case managers—the system's linchpins—are leaving en masse. While regulations limit each case manager to 120 clients, many handle over 150. Shih Sheng-mao from Red Heart Foundation says: "Social workers serving isolated elderly handle 30-60 cases with deep engagement. With 150 cases, comprehensive care becomes impossible."

A dark joke circulates in care circles: "Our future long-term care depends on two types of people—foreigners and robots!"

## The Invisible Half: 240,000 Foreign Caregivers Supporting a Parallel World

While Long-Term Care 2.0 garnered media praise, another massive care system quietly expanded.

Taiwan opened to foreign domestic caregivers in 1992 with just 306 workers. By 2024, this number reached 214,514—80% from Indonesia, 10.8% from the Philippines, 8.9% from Vietnam. Lined up, they would stretch from Taipei to Kaohsiung.

> **📊 Data Source**
> According to Ministry of Labor statistics, as of March 2024, Taiwan has 910,000 people over 80, with 41.7% experiencing some level of disability.

These 214,000 foreign caregiver households form a care world completely parallel to LTC 2.0. Employing a foreign caregiver costs roughly NT$30,000 monthly for 24-hour care; in contrast, LTC 2.0's maximum monthly subsidy for severe disability is only NT$32,340, with limited service hours.

More critically, these systems rarely intersect. Current regulations prevent households with foreign caregivers from accessing most LTC 2.0 services (except assistive devices and home accessibility improvements). This exclusion clause makes 210,000 households institutional islands.

> **⚠️ Controversial View**
> Starting 2026, LTC 3.0 will include foreign caregiver households in community services, but the Ministry of Health and Welfare fears this could spike migrant worker demand by 160,000, creating supply shortages.

## 24-Hour vs. 8-Hour: Battle of Two Care Philosophies

Why do Taiwan families pay NT$30,000 for foreign caregivers instead of using subsidized LTC 2.0?

In Hualien, 69-year-old Lien Jin-ying cares for her 98-year-old mother with dementia while also tending to her 76-year-old husband. She explains: "Long-term care (home services) only comes 2 hours daily. I rush home to cook for my husband during that time, then return by 1 PM. Evenings, basically no one is available."

This illustrates the fundamental difference: LTC 2.0 provides "segmented professional care," while foreign caregivers offer "round-the-clock life companionship." The former meets international professional standards; the latter meets East Asian family needs.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's care model choice reflects a struggle between Nordic "professionalized, community-based" values versus East Asian "familial, holistic" approaches.

Lynn Yu Ling Ng from National University of Singapore, after comparing Taiwan and Singapore care models, found Taiwan's foreign caregivers function more as "family care assistants" than "family care replacements." This stems from Taiwan's strict application requirements—medical assessments are mandatory, forcing employers to learn basic care skills and remain involved even after hiring foreign help.

In contrast, LTC 2.0's professional services are often absent during peak need hours. Wei Li-hu, among the few caregivers specializing in night shifts, notes: "Most people still refuse night work despite NT$385 differential pay."

## Assembly Line Dignity: When Care Becomes Rush Work

Labor shortages exact costs beyond scheduling difficulties—they compromise care quality.

Residential care social worker Zhang Kai-jie (pseudonym) observes: Even basic services like bathing become assembly-line operations. "8 AM bathing in winter at 10-plus degrees Celsius, pulling residents from warm beds. They queue outside bathrooms while three caregivers divide tasks: one undresses, one supports bodies, one washes."

Yang Ming Chiao Tung University assistant professor Li Shao-fen terms this phenomenon "care rush work"—caregivers must numb their senses daily to complete labor tasks, limiting their care agency while residents bear the risks.

> **💡 Did You Know?**
> Most residential care facilities only bathe residents twice weekly in winter due to insufficient staff to provide services at appropriate times.

Former residential caregiver Xiao Shi (pseudonym) recalls: "Bath time meant washing 10-plus beds, rotating every 2-3 hours—constant cleaning, feeding, bathing. Very assembly-line, very rushed. After bathing, I'd be drenched in sweat."

This "efficiency-first" care model partly explains why Taiwan families choose foreign caregivers: at home, basic dignity can still be preserved.

## International Perspective: Taiwan's Unique Model

Taiwan's dual-track care system is internationally distinctive.

Germany established long-term care insurance in 1994 using a "cash or services" model—recipients choose between monetary benefits for self-arranged care or professional services. Japan's care insurance centers on community-integrated service centers emphasizing unified medical, care, and housing services. The Netherlands established long-term care insurance in 1968, now providing standardized services through "care packages."

No country parallels Taiwan's approach: simultaneously operating two equally-sized, mutually incompatible care systems.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's long-term care contradictions reflect East Asian societies' modernization struggles: Do we want professionalized Nordic models or humanized family models? The answer is both, but we're executing neither well.

Scholar Liang Li-fang notes: "Taiwan's foreign caregivers were never meant to replace family roles but supplement family care deficiencies. This aligns with Confucian cultural expectations of family responsibility."

But this model's cost is institutional fragmentation. The 210,000 households employing foreign caregivers cannot access LTC 2.0's community resources, respite services, or assistive support. They live beyond institutional visibility, surviving alone.

## Three Arrows of Rescue: Government Response to Labor Crisis

Facing long-term care workforce shortages, the Ministry of Health and Welfare launched three policy arrows:

**First Arrow: Multi-faceted Companion Care Service Pilot Program**
Allows citizens to purchase temporary, short-term care services. Three months after launching in 2025, 15 counties served 485 clients, demonstrating genuine demand.

**Second Arrow: Industry-Academia Cooperation International Program**
Recruits Vietnamese and Indonesian students for two-year long-term care programs with three-year employment contracts. The first cohort of 22 students has been fully reserved by industry; the second cohort will expand to 40.

**Third Arrow: Opening to Mid-level Foreign Technical Talent**
Foreign caregivers with 6+ years experience earning NT$29,000+ monthly can become "mid-level technical workers" without time restrictions. As of February 2024, 15,000 people have obtained this status.

> **⚠️ Controversial View**
> The three arrows appear substantial, but 22 Vietnamese students against hundreds of thousands in workforce demand has been criticized as "a drop in the bucket." Real solutions may require raising social status and wages for care services.

## LTC 3.0's Gamble: Integration or Greater Fragmentation?

Starting 2026, LTC 3.0 will phase in, with the biggest change being inclusion of foreign caregiver households in community services. Will this policy resolve dual-system contradictions or complicate problems further?

Ministry of Health and Welfare Deputy Minister Lu Chien-te states: "Foreign workers are supplementary labor. When local workers won't do it, foreigners do." But reality shows 214,000 foreign caregivers are no longer "supplementary"—they're Taiwan's care system backbone.

If LTC 3.0 successfully integrates both systems, Taiwan could create a world-unique "hybrid care model" combining professional service quality standards with family care's human warmth.

If it fails, Taiwan may face more severe institutional fragmentation: wealthy families employing foreign caregivers enjoy dual services while others queue for government resources, amplifying class differences during the most vulnerable care moments.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's long-term care future ultimately answers a deeper question: What kind of society do we want to become? An efficiency-first professional society, or a warmth-prioritizing humanistic society?

## Epilogue: Two Ways of Love

86-year-old Mr. Tong Fu-chien from Tainan has kidney disease and dementia. His Indonesian caregiver Astuti daily grinds soft tofu and noodles for him because "Grandpa can't chew well but likes noodles." Astuti calls him "yi ji bang" (first-rate); his son considers Astuti "like another daughter."

Meanwhile, 69-year-old Lien Jin-ying in Hualien cares for her 98-year-old mother with dementia, using LTC 2.0 home services for meal assistance while sleeping on the floor in her mother's room at night. She says: "This is my mother! I cannot neglect her."

These two stories represent Taiwan society's dual understanding of "care": one is professional division of labor with controllable service quality; the other is holistic companionship as family extension.

Taiwan's long-term care development seeks balance between these two expressions of love. We're still learning how to maintain human warmth while ensuring professional quality; how to pursue efficiency without forgetting that care's essence is human-to-human connection.

This learning process will continue for a long time. But at least, we've begun.
